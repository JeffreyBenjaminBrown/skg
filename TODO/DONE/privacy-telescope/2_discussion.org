(Rewritten 2026-07-11 against the expanded vision that arrived with
the pull; the first draft critiqued the earlier, shorter vision.)

* Summary
The expanded vision settles most of what the first draft flagged as
open, and settles it well (see [[*What the new draft settles][the next section]]). What remains:

1. *Direction inversions in the text.* The publicness invariant, the
   auto-defined source-set example, and two =F(j-i)= subscripts all
   point the wrong way relative to the worked example and to each
   other. Mechanical fixes, but they are the load-bearing lines of
   the spec. See [[*Incorrect things][Incorrect things]].
2. *One concept is missing a name and a type*: the distinction
   between the chain's one real, user-visible node (I will call it
   the *perceived node*) and the invisible, auto-managed *carriers*.
   Half the open questions below dissolve once that distinction is
   explicit. See [[*TODO Perceived node vs carrier: name it, type it][the carrier section]].
3. *The biggest danger is silent, not crashy*: a bug or ambiguity in
   the save-time chain renormalization moves memberships between
   privacy levels without anyone noticing, and every inbound query
   (ancestry, container counts, cols) will leak carrier IDs into
   view unless carrier-transparency is systematic. See
   [[*Dangerous things][Dangerous things]].
4. *Two expressiveness gaps are now baked in* and should be either
   accepted aloud or given escape hatches: a private membership of a
   public or foreign child is inexpressible under
   source-determines-membership, and title/body edits always land at
   the perceived node's own (most public) level. See
   [[*Unmade decisions][Unmade decisions]].
5. The "extends the chain on the other side" case (inserting a child
   more public than the base) does not hold up under analysis; I
   recommend dropping it. See [[*TODO "Extends the chain on the other side" seems wrong; recommend capping at the base][that section]].

* What the new draft settles
Recorded so the prior draft's warnings are visibly discharged:

- *(likely solved): privacy-monotone chains.* The prior draft's top
  danger — "distinct sources" being too weak, letting a chain leak
  private IDs into more-public files — is now addressed by the
  concentric privacy order plus the monotonicity invariant (modulo
  the inverted sentence, below).
- *(likely solved): stored-relative placement.* The worked example
  (f2's representation changes while f1's does not) pins the
  decision the prior draft called load-bearing: the placement is the
  stored representation, so base edits propagate automatically,
  including edits arriving by git pull.
- *(likely solved): visible portions are prefixes.* Concentric
  source-sets make the visible part of a monotone chain a prefix of
  it, which is exactly what the fold needs — the prior draft's
  "gaps in the visible sub-chain" problem dissolves structurally
  (given the prefix reading of source sets; see
  [[*TODO The auto-defined source-set example contradicts its own selection UI][the inversion issue]]).
- *(likely solved): membership attribution.* The child's source
  decides which link records the membership. A good default; two
  consequences flagged below.
- *(likely solved): the placement type.* Rejecting the uniform
  list-of-(maybe-anchor, ids) in favor of a pair is right; two small
  nits in [[*(likely solved): the placement pair type — two nits][the type section]].
- *(likely solved): no override machinery involved.* In the worked
  example the buffer draws f1 — the base — with folded-in children.
  So autofork rendering is content *augmentation* of the perceived
  node, not override *substitution* of it. That quietly discharges a
  whole family of the prior draft's worries (=overridesHere=
  markers, the tamper check, collected-id round-tripping, monogamy
  interplay) — provided the independence decision below is confirmed.

* Incorrect things
** TODO The publicness invariant is inverted
The special-case conditions say "If i > j, then i is more public
than j". The worked example says the opposite: F2 (index 2) lives in
S2, "more private than S1". The example is clearly the intent
(forkward = more private; the base is the public face). The
invariant should read: if i > j, then Fi's source is more *private*
than Fj's.

** TODO The auto-defined source-set example contradicts its own selection UI
The example defines suffixes of the privacy order:
"trusted-or-more-private = (trusted, private)". But:
- A set that *excludes* =public= is useless for the privacy
  use-case: screen-sharing with trusted friends should show public
  and trusted notes, not trusted and private ones.
- The UI sentence — "ask the user to select the most private source
  to make available" — describes a *prefix*: selecting =trusted=
  yields {public, trusted}.
- The chain machinery *requires* the prefix reading: a fold cannot
  render F2 without F1, so the visible portion of a chain must be a
  prefix, which prefix sets guarantee and suffix sets destroy.
So the intended family is surely {S1..Sk}, "everything at most as
private as Sk". The set names then read "X-or-more-public", or
better, drop names entirely as the vision suggests and speak only of
the selected level.

** F(j-i) is presumably F(j-1), twice
"Fj hides nothing from F(j-i)" and "Fj maintains the order of
F(j-i)'s content". Either =F(j-1)= is meant (sufficient — the
constraint propagates transitively down the chain), or "F(j-k) for
all k > 0" (the anchors line already uses that form). Worth fixing
because these two lines are the definition.

** TODO "Extends the chain on the other side" seems wrong; recommend capping at the base
The vision: inserting a child from a source *more public* than the
base's "extends the chain on the other side" — a new link basewards
of f1. I do not think this case survives analysis:

- The point of putting a membership in link L is that readers at
  L's level can see it. But the *container* is only visible from
  f1's level upward — a reader too public to see f1 cannot see any
  child-of-f1 relationship anyway, wherever it is stored. A
  basewards carrier F0 would expose, to the most public audience, a
  titleless orphan node containing the child: pure noise, no
  function.
- It also breaks the shape of the chain: the perceived node stops
  being the base, so the most public representative of the node is
  a carrier with no title — colliding with "autofork nodes are not
  represented to the user at all".
- A membership can never usefully be more public than the less
  public of (container, child).

Recommended rule instead: membership level = the more private of
(base's source, child's source); a child more public than the base
just lands in the base's own =contains=. Recording a more-public ID
in a more-private file leaks nothing.

** TODO Two conflicting rules for foreign sources
"Foreign sources can be interspersed [in the config's privacy
order] harmlessly if the user chooses" (source-set section) versus
"Non-owned sources are treated as maximally public" (chain
section). These answer different questions and should be split
explicitly:
- *Visibility*: which levels show a foreign source? Interspersal is
  the right tool — the user places each foreign source at a chosen
  position, and prefix selection includes it from that level on.
  (The practical case for a non-top position: hiding someone's
  edgier notes while screen-sharing.) "Maximally public" as a
  visibility rule would force every foreign source into every
  level, foreclosing that.
- *Chain geometry*: where may a foreign node sit in a chain? Only
  at the base (F1), per "F1 can be owned or not; the rest must be
  owned" — for this purpose "foreign counts as basewards of
  everything owned" is fine.
Also note the vision's own hedge ("this decision might bite me
later") is about the right thing: the bite arrives exactly when a
foreign source is more sensitive than some owned source, which the
interspersal reading handles and the maximally-public reading
cannot.

* Unmade decisions
** TODO Does 'autofork' decompose into subscribes + overrides, or stand alone?
The vocab section still defines fork as "subscribes to and
overrides", but the sixth-relationship section never says whether an
autofork edge *implies* those two edges, and the worked example's
rendering (base drawn, children folded) uses neither: no
substitution, no unintegrated-content col. Two coherent readings:
- *Independent* (recommended): 'autofork' is its own relation; a
  carrier neither subscribes to nor overrides its forkee. Rendering
  is the fold; the override and subscription systems are untouched.
  Fewer implied facts, no col/stat noise to suppress, no monogamy
  interplay. The vocab section then needs a note that autofork is
  a *third* thing, not a fork in the defined sense.
- *Decomposing*: the machinery of subscriptions (subscribeeCols,
  hides) and overrides (substitution, monogamy, cycle checks,
  tamper check) all acquire in-chain cases that mostly need
  suppressing. I see no benefit purchased by that cost.
Since the vision says "my thinking on it is likely incomplete", this
is the one place I would spend the next design hour: everything
downstream (validators, cols, heralds, save extraction) branches on
this answer.

** TODO Perceived node vs carrier: name it, type it
The vision calls both f1 and f2 "autofork nodes" but treats them
utterly differently: f1 is a real node (title, body, user-visible,
movable); f2 is invisible, unmovable, auto-created and auto-deleted.
Making the distinction first-class answers a raft of questions
mechanically:
- A chain has exactly one perceived node (the base — assuming
  [[*TODO "Extends the chain on the other side" seems wrong; recommend capping at the base][the basewards case]] is dropped); carriers hold only placements.
- Title, body, aliases of the perceived node live in its file; a
  carrier has none. (Format wrinkle: =title= is mandatory in .skg
  files today — carriers need either an exemption or a generated
  placeholder; decide which.)
- Every enumerable surface must decide whether carriers appear:
  Tantivy/search (surely not), 'titles by ids' (no), export-to-org
  (fold instead), nodeMerge acquirer/acquiree (refuse), the
  override-choice menu (never), dired-style file views (unavoidable
  — they are files). A =CarrierNode= type (or a flag on
  'NodeComplete') turns each of these into a compiler-visible
  decision instead of an accident.
- It also gives the exclusion validator its subject: a carrier with
  a body, an explicit subscribes/overrides edge, or an inbound
  textlink is malformed data to report.

** TODO The renormalization algorithm needs a spec of its own
The save-side is where the vision's complexity concentrates: from
(the perceived child list, each child's membership level, the
previous chain) compute the new chain — creating links (F1.5
appears), destroying them ("ceases to be"), re-anchoring forkward
placements when anchors move or change level. Requirements worth
stating up front, because they are correctness properties, not
implementation details:
- *pure and unit-testable*: one module owning the transform, the
  way 'weave' owns invisible-member preservation;
- *deterministic and idempotent*: saving twice changes nothing;
- *stable*: reuse existing carrier IDs and minimize placement
  churn — otherwise every save rewrites carrier files and the
  user's git history becomes unreadable;
- *total*: junk input (hand-edited or foreign) degrades to warnings.
Also two rules the algorithm needs that the vision leaves implicit:
- *anchor selection*: when a run must re-anchor (its anchor was
  deleted, or an intermediate link appeared — the vision's own
  "might change the anchor points" case), which anchor is chosen?
  "Nearest preceding member of the chainward fold" is the natural
  rule; pin it.
- *the same-anchor tie-break*: if two links anchor runs to the same
  ID, fold order makes the more-private run land closer to the
  anchor. Whatever the choice, it is user-visible order; pin it.

** TODO Which level records hides, subscriptions, and aliases?
Child-source decides =contains= membership. The other writable
surfaces of the perceived node need their own attribution rule:
- *subscriptions*: the invariant text says collections
  ("esp. subscriptions") fold, so a private subscription of a
  public node is expressible — good, that is a real want. Rule for
  which link records a new subscription: the subscribee's source
  level (mirroring contains) seems right.
- *hides*: a hide references the hidden node but is also a fact
  about your curation; where it is recorded is observable on disk.
  The child-source rule would make hiding a public subscribee-child
  a public act; the user might want the hide itself private. Needs
  a decision (and it interacts with hides being per-hider, not
  per-subscription).
- *aliases have no source to inherit from* — they are strings, not
  nodes, so the child-source rule cannot apply at all. Default to
  the viewing level? Explicit metadata? This one cannot be dodged;
  a private alias for a public node ("the client I complain about"
  → some public company node) is exactly the kind of thing users
  will create.

** TODO Text edits always land at the base: the silent-public-edit hazard
The perceived node's title and body live in its own file at its own
(most public) level. So a user at the private level, editing what
looks like a private view, writes a public file when they retitle
the node or edit its body. That is arguably correct — it IS the
public node — but it breaks the intuition "private mode ⇒ my edits
are private". Options: warn in the minibuffer when a save writes a
file more public than the viewing level; or document it and rely on
the diff view. Related boundary to document: privately *diverging*
text (a different private title) is precisely what autofork does
not do — that remains the manual fork system's job.

** TODO Private membership of a public or foreign child needs an escape hatch
Under source-determined membership, a foreign child (foreign =
maximally public) always lands basewards — so a *private reading
list of public materials*, a core use case, is inexpressible, even
though the data model supports it perfectly well (a placement may
list any IDs). Recommend: keep child-source as the default, allow
per-child metadata to *raise* the membership's privacy (never
lower it — a membership more public than its child is the ID leak
this whole design exists to prevent; the validator should enforce
membershipLevel ≥ childLevel).

** TODO Carrier maintenance should run source-set-ungated at save
A save from a restricted level can delete or move a node that some
invisible carrier uses as an anchor. The server can see every file
regardless of the connection's active level (source-sets gate
display, not server capability), so the save *can* re-anchor the
affected carriers immediately. If it does not, dangling anchors
accumulate until the user next saves at full visibility. Recommend
maintain-always; the alternative needs a deterministic render-time
fallback anyway (below), but there is no reason to let the debt
build.

** TODO Interaction with the manual fork system on the same node
Both systems will touch one node: f1 carries an AF chain *and* the
user manually forks f1 (wanting a divergent private title — the
general case the vision explicitly leaves manual). The manual clone
copies f1's contains at fork time: the *stored* list or the
*perceived* (folded) one? (Perceived, presumably — the user forked
what they saw; the restatement is fine because the clone is more
private.) And when substitution then draws the manual clone D in
f1's place, D's own AF chain (if any) folds into D. Probably all
consistent; needs a paragraph in the spec and a test, not a design
change.

** TODO What replaces named source-sets, and what happens to the features gated on them
Auto-defined levels replace manual =[[source_sets]]= config
(no-legacy: delete the old mechanism, per
[[../../coding-advice/for-ai.md][for-ai.md]]). Loose ends: the reserved set =all= and the features
that require it (git diff mode, diff analysis) map naturally to
"maximum level plus every foreign source", but
[[../../api-and-formats.md][api-and-formats.md]] should say so; =default_source_set= becomes a
default *level*; and the per-connection reset-on-reconnect behavior
carries over unchanged.

** TODO Migration
Two kinds of existing data predate the feature:
- direct cross-privacy memberships (a public node whose =contains=
  names a private ID — the documented leak caveat in
  [[../../docs/private-writing.md][private-writing.md]] territory): auto-convert on next save?
  A sweep command? Leave until touched?
- manual owned forks created while dogfooding 'skg-fork-node':
  convertible to AF chains only when they satisfy the invariants
  (no reorder, no hides, no text divergence); a converter should
  check and report rather than assume.

** TODO Inspectability of carriers
"Not represented to the user at all" is right as a default and
wrong as an absolute: when something goes wrong (a dangling anchor
warning, a git-archaeology session, "why is this child here?"),
the user needs *some* sanctioned window onto the chain — even if it
is just a documented read-only debug view or a documented file
layout. Deciding "git and the filesystem are the debug interface"
is a legitimate answer; deciding nothing is not.

** TODO Pin the AFk herald's no-leak rule and its k
- k should count only *visible* links, and the herald should be
  suppressed when k = 1: a visible chain of one link is
  indistinguishable from no chain, and emitting AF1 would tell a
  public-level onlooker that a more private layer exists — the
  exact leak the architecture otherwise avoids (nothing in a public
  file references the private layer; the herald must not become the
  one channel that does).
- Also pin whether k counts links or nodes, i.e. whether the
  perceived node itself counts (AF2 vs AF1 for base-plus-one-fork).

* Dangerous things
** Carrier transparency must be systematic, not per-feature
On disk, c2's container is F2, not f1. Every inbound-relation
surface — containerward ancestry, 'contained_by' and the other
inverse indexes, container counts in graphNodeStats, backpaths,
inbound PartnerCols, search-result enrichment ancestry — will
surface carrier IDs where the user expects the perceived node.
Handling this feature-by-feature guarantees missed spots (diff
phantoms, export, the col matrix...). The codebase already has the
right pattern: extra-ids make many IDs present as one node, resolved
at the accessor layer via 'pid_of'. Carriers want the same
treatment — a resolution applied wherever 'pid_of' already is, so
that no consumer can forget. This is the largest single piece of
implementation risk in the feature.

** Renormalization bugs move memberships between privacy levels silently
The failure mode of a wrong chain-normalization is not a crash; it
is a membership quietly landing one level more public than the user
intended. That argues for:
- property tests on the normalize/fold pair (fold(normalize(view))
  == view; every membership's level == the rule's answer; normalize
  is idempotent and stable);
- a =ChainInvariantViolation= validator enforced at both gates
  (save and init/rebuild) through one shared primitive — the
  override-invariants lesson already recorded in
  [[../problems.org][TODO/problems.org]] (two divergent validators nearly let bad data
  through) applies verbatim here;
- the invariants to check: distinct sources, privacy monotonicity,
  autofork acyclicity, carrier well-formedness (no body, no
  explicit sharing edges, exactly one forkee), anchor resolvability.

** Dangling anchors: the fold must be total and deterministic
The base can change while nobody is folding — another machine, a
git pull, a hand edit. If an anchor ID has been deleted (or merged:
anchors must resolve through 'pid_of', since nodeMerges rename
IDs), the fold must neither error nor guess randomly: fall back
deterministically (attach the orphaned run after the previous run,
or at START if it is first), emit a completion warning, and let the
next save renormalize. Same spirit as 'weave''s
cling-to-the-nearest-surviving-anchor rule, coarsened to what a
placement can know.

** A chain is only readable if it can be folded
Two consequences of stored-relative placement to design for
explicitly:
- *Foreign chains must fold.* A colleague who shares two sources
  (their public and trusted layers) ships carrier files; rendering
  their perceived nodes requires folding *their* chain. So the fold
  is format-level, not an owner privilege — only chain *editing* is
  ownership-gated. (Folding a foreign chain needs no knowledge of
  the foreign author's config: the autofork edges themselves give
  the order.)
- *A missing base is a new failure mode.* A partial clone of
  someone's sources, or a mid-pull git state, can leave a carrier
  whose forkee file is absent — the node's effective content is
  then uncomputable. Define the degradation (render the surviving
  placements with a loud warning; never panic), analogous to
  dangling =contains= refs degrading to phantoms today.

** Deleting or moving the perceived node
- Deleting f1: carriers point at it (the autofork edge lives on the
  fork). 'apply_delete_propagation_cleanup' must decide: destroy
  the whole chain (deleting the carriers and their memberships —
  drastic), or refuse until the user empties it. Silently scrubbing
  the edge and leaving orphan carriers is the one wrong answer.
- Moving f1 to a different source recomputes the chain (the vision
  says so); note the move can invert monotonicity against an
  existing carrier (base moved *more private than* a carrier), so
  the recomputation must re-derive every membership's level, not
  just relabel the base.

* Awkward, inelegant, or overly complex things
** The chain-splitting lifecycle is the accepted complexity center — confine it
Links appearing (F1.5), disappearing, and re-anchoring their
forkward neighbors is inherent to the design and fine — but only if
it lives in one pure module (the normalization above) rather than
being smeared across the save pipeline's stages. The precedent to
imitate is how 'weave' isolates invisible-member preservation:
callers hand it lists; it owns the semantics.

** (likely solved): the placement pair type — two nits
Rejecting =list of (maybe anchor, ids)= for a pair — (maybe prepend,
list of (anchor, ids)) — is the right instinct; two residues:
- =Option= on the prepend list buys nothing: "no prepend" and
  "empty prepend" mean the same thing in storage, so a plain
  possibly-empty list is simpler. (The MSV-style
  absent-vs-empty distinction earns its keep for save *intents*,
  not for this on-disk field.)
- Invalid states remain even in the pair: duplicate anchors, runs
  out of chainward order, empty runs, an anchor that is also an
  inserted ID. Normalization plus validation is still needed;
  full unrepresentability is not reachable here.
(Also a typo: "list of (anchor, list of tuple) pairs" — "list of
ids" is meant.)

** Prior art to reuse rather than reinvent
- 'weave' ([[../../server/from_text/weave.rs][weave.rs]]): runs-keyed-by-anchor merging — the fold's
  sibling, and the deterministic-fallback precedent.
- 'resolve_override' ([[../../server/dbs/in_rust_graph/override_resolution.rs][override_resolution.rs]]): the shape of a
  guarded walk with a cycle backstop, for the chain resolver.
- 'pid_of' / extra-ids: the many-IDs-one-presented-node mechanism,
  for carrier transparency.
- The two-gate validator pattern
  ([[../../server/dbs/in_rust_graph/override_invariants.rs][override_invariants.rs]]) with one shared primitive.
- The =NodeRelation= enum ([[../../server/dbs/in_rust_graph/relation_accessors.rs][relation_accessors.rs]]): adding
  =Autoforks= as a sixth variant makes the compiler enumerate every
  place that must decide what autofork means for it — the cheapest
  systematic-coverage tool available. (For what it is worth, the
  earlier vision's TypeDB-rules idea is moot regardless: TypeDB 3.x
  removed rules, and its functions must be invoked per query. A
  sixth relation plus Rust-side folding, as now proposed, is the
  right call; TypeDB, being a disk-rebuilt cache, can simply be fed
  whatever derived edges prove useful.)

** Minor: one word, three referents
"Autofork" currently names the relation, the carriers, and the
chain; the perceived node has no name at all. Suggested vocabulary
for the docs: *autofork edge*, *carrier*, *chain*, *perceived
node*. Cheap now, expensive to retrofit into error messages and
tests later.

** Minor: herald placement claim
"Part of the first group of orange heralds. (The second group is
blue.)" — cross-check against the actual rule table in
[[../../server/heralds.rs][server/heralds.rs]] and the herald-legibility plan in
[[../../TODO.org][TODO.org]] (which assigns yellow/blue to leading/later relations
and orange to "unusual things") before this sentence hardens.

* Efficiency notes (minor)
- Chains are as short as the user's privacy ladder (2–4 links);
  folds are trivial. The only care is cache invalidation: a write
  to any chain member invalidates the folds of its forkward suffix.
- Renormalization is O(perceived children) per save; fine.
- Collateral rerendering must treat a carrier write as a change to
  the perceived node (transparency again), or views of f1 at other
  levels go stale after a private-child edit.
- A pleasant side effect worth keeping in the pitch: placement
  storage makes private git diffs smaller and quieter — a public
  reorder touches no private file.
